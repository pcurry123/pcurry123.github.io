About Us

Overgrowth-intellectual disability (OGID) syndromes are a group of conditions characterized by increased height and/or head circumference (≥2SD) in association with an intellectual disability. This study, led by Professor Kate Tatton-Brown, has recruited over 2000 patients with OGID, identified new genes (DNMT3A, EZH2, PPP2R5D, and HIST1H1E), defined new clinical syndromes and translated the findings into clinical practice. The work, in partnership with St. George’s NHS Trust, will be using the very latest genomic technologies and bioinformatics to investigate new genetic causes of OGID syndromes and define associated clinical presentations and natural history of these conditions. These findings in turn will be integrated into clinical practice, informing patient management and genetic testing opportunities through NHS diagnostic laboratories.

Meet The Team

Professor Kate Tatton-Brown

Project Lead

Professor Kate Tatton-Brown is a consultant in clinical genetics. She has a particular interest in paediatric genetic disorders, growth disorders, endocrine disorders and paediatric cancer genetics.

Dr Kate Everett

Project Lead

Dr Everett's research focuses primarily on neurogenetics, with an emphasis on identifying the genetic cause of neurodevelopmental disorders. Since joining St George's in 2010, she has established research links with the clinical genetics team at St George's University Hospital NHS Trust.

Dr Alan Pittman

Bioinformatician

Dr Alan Pittman is a lecturer in Bioinformatics at St. Georges University of London. He has broad research interests ranging from human genomics, rare-diseases and population genetics, with a particular emphasis on omics technologies and their applications, including, whole-exome sequencing, whole-genome sequencing, genetic mosaicism detection and functional genomics.

Dr Chris Carroll

Scientist Collaborator

Dr Chris Carroll and his team use CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing technologies to gain a more complete understanding of peadiatric diseases, particularly focussing on mitochondrial disorders.

Philippa Curry

Research assistant

Philippa Curry is a research assistant working on the project, focussing on investigating the genetics of OGID syndromes.

Jesitha Sivananthan

University home page

St George's, University of London

“Genetics and genomics are vital and cross-cutting topics in a 21st-century biomedical university. St George’s has been expanding this theme, along with the underlying expertise provided by our Bioinformatics Centre. Genetics and genomics research in the MCS Institute includes internationally leading work on the cardiovascular system, notably on sudden death; and the lymphovascular system. Other eminent groups study genetics of tissue overgrowth, pigmentary disorders and skin cancer; mitochondrial disorders (which can affect the brain and heart); muscular dystrophies, developmental disorders, and headache. Molecular-genetic techniques are used to study molecules involved in organelle synthesis and function, DNA replication, cancer drug toxicity and the action of potential novel antibiotics.”
